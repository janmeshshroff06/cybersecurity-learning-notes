# 1.3 Risk and Asset Security

## Overview

This section covers the importance of security planning, risk management, organizational policies, and the NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF). Security is a shared responsibility that helps organizations protect assets, reduce risks, and maintain business operations.

---

## Elements of a Security Plan

### Security as a Shared Responsibility

Security involves:

- People
- Processes
- Technology

Effective security plans help organizations:

- Protect assets
- Manage risks
- Build a security-focused culture

Security requires participation from:

- Employees
- Vendors
- Customers
- Leadership

---

## Security as a Culture

Security is not only the responsibility of IT teams.

Everyone in an organization contributes to protecting assets and reducing risk.

### Goals

- Protect physical assets
- Protect digital assets
- Reduce organizational risk
- Promote secure behavior

---

## Purpose of Security Plans

Organizations create security plans to:

- Prepare for risks before they occur
- Improve communication and collaboration
- Reduce the likelihood of security incidents

### Focus Areas

- People
- Communication
- Collaboration
- Risk reduction

---

## Risk Categories

Risks can negatively impact the CIA Triad:

- Confidentiality
- Integrity
- Availability

### Common Risk Categories

- Damage of information
- Disclosure of information
- Loss of information

### Risk Factors

- Physical damage
- Hardware failures
- Cyberattacks
- Human error

### Example

Sending sensitive company information through a personal email account.

---

## Communicating Security Plans

Organizations communicate security expectations through:

- Policies
- Standards
- Procedures

These form the foundation of organizational security planning.

---

## Policies

### Definition

A policy is a set of rules designed to reduce risk and protect assets.

### Policies Answer Questions Such As

- What are we protecting?
- Why are we protecting it?
- Who is responsible?

### Focus

- Strategy
- Goals
- Scope
- Organizational expectations

### Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)

Defines acceptable use of organizational systems and resources.

Often reviewed and signed by new employees.

### Purpose

- Establish security expectations
- Define acceptable behavior

---

## Standards

### Definition

Standards provide security requirements and points of reference.

### Purpose

- Support policy implementation
- Improve consistency across the organization

### Example

NIST Special Publication 800-63B

### Sample Requirement

Passwords must contain at least eight characters.

---

## Procedures

### Definition

Procedures are step-by-step instructions explaining how tasks should be completed.

### Examples

- Creating secure passwords
- Resetting passwords
- Following incident response steps

### Benefits

- Accountability
- Consistency
- Efficiency

---

## Customized Security Plans

Security plans vary between organizations.

### Factors Influencing Security Plans

- Business objectives
- Organizational risks
- Industry requirements
- Operational needs

### Overview

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework helps organizations:

- Standards

### Overview

NIST develops:

- Security standards
- Security frameworks
- Best practices

---

## NIST Cybersecurity Framework Components

The CSF consists of three primary components:

- Core
- Tiers
- Profiles

---

## CSF Core

### Overview

The Core contains the major functions of a cybersecurity program.

### Five Functions

#### Identify

Understand:

- Assets
- Risks
- Vulnerabilities

Supports:

- Asset management
- Risk assessment

#### Protect

Implement safeguards that reduce risk.

Examples:

- Access controls
- Security awareness training
- Data protection measures

#### Detect

Identify cybersecurity events as quickly as possible.

Focus Areas:

- Monitoring
- Threat detection
- Visibility

#### Respond

Take action when a security incident occurs.

Examples:

- Incident response
- Containment
- Communication

#### Recover

Restore systems and operations after an incident.

Focus Areas:

- Recovery planning
- Business continuity
- System restoration

---

## CSF Tiers

### Purpose

Measure cybersecurity maturity and effectiveness.

### Tier Range

#### Tier 1 – Passive

- Minimal cybersecurity maturity
- Basic security practices

#### Tier 4 – Adaptive

- Advanced cybersecurity maturity
- Continuous improvement
- Strong security integration

### Benefits

- Measure performance
- Identify weaknesses
- Guide security improvements

---

## CSF Profiles

### Definition

Profiles provide a snapshot of an organization's current cybersecurity posture.

### Purpose

- Compare current and desired security states
- Identify gaps
- Support planning and decision-making

### Benefits

- Measure progress over time
- Guide continuous improvement

---

## Key Takeaways

- Security plans are built around policies, standards, and procedures.
- Security is a shared responsibility involving people, processes, and technology.
- Risks can affect confidentiality, integrity, and availability.
- Compliance ensures organizations follow standards and regulations.
- The NIST Cybersecurity Framework helps organizations manage cybersecurity risk.
- The CSF consists of Core, Tiers, and Profiles.
- The five Core functions are:
  - Identify
  - Protect
  - Detect
  - Respond
  - Recover
- Tiers measure cybersecurity maturity.
- Profiles provide a snapshot of an organization's security posture.

---

## Big Picture

Risk management begins with understanding assets and building effective security plans. Policies, standards, and procedures establish organizational expectations, while the NIST Cybersecurity Framework provides a structured approach to identifying risks, protecting assets, detecting threats, responding to incidents, and recovering from disruptions. Together, these practices help organizations reduce risk and improve cybersecurity resilience.
