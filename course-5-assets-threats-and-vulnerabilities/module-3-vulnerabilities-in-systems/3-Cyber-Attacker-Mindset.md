# **3.3 Cyber Attacker Mindset**

---

## **Protect All Entry Points**

---

### **Overview**

* Organizations must understand and protect their **attack surface**.

* The larger the attack surface:

  * The more opportunities attackers have

* Security teams use:

  * Security hardening
  * Risk assessments
  * Security controls

* Goal:

  * Reduce vulnerabilities
  * Reduce potential attack paths

---

### **Attack Surface**

* An **attack surface** is all possible vulnerabilities that a threat actor could exploit.

* Includes:

  * Physical attack surfaces
  * Digital attack surfaces

---

### **Physical Attack Surface**

* Physical assets that attackers can access or manipulate.

**Examples:**

* Laptops
* Servers
* Mobile devices
* Employees
* Office facilities

---

### **Digital Attack Surface**

* Systems and resources connected to networks.

**Examples:**

* Websites
* Cloud services
* Email systems
* Applications
* Databases

---

### **Cloud Computing and Attack Surfaces**

* Cloud services increase accessibility.

* Also increase the number of:

  * Potential vulnerabilities
  * Potential entry points

* Organizations must secure assets accessible from anywhere.

---

### **Security Hardening**

* The process of reducing vulnerabilities and strengthening systems.

**Goals:**

* Reduce attack surface
* Limit entry points
* Improve system security

---

### **Examples of Security Hardening**

* Removing unnecessary software
* Disabling unused services
* Applying security patches
* Enforcing strong authentication
* Restricting permissions

---

## **Approach Cybersecurity with an Attacker Mindset**

---

### **Overview**

* Security professionals often think like attackers.

* Helps identify weaknesses before threat actors discover them.

* Focuses on understanding:

  * Targets
  * Attack methods
  * Opportunities for exploitation

---

### **Attacker Mindset**

* A method of analyzing systems from an attacker's perspective.

* Questions security professionals ask:

  * What is valuable?
  * How could someone gain access?
  * Which defenses are weakest?

---

### **Benefits**

* Helps identify vulnerabilities.

* Improves security planning.

* Strengthens defenses proactively.

---

### **Attacker Mindset Process**

#### **1. Identify the Target**

* Determine what an attacker wants.

**Examples:**

* Sensitive data
* Credentials
* Financial information
* Critical systems

---

#### **2. Determine Access Opportunities**

* Identify information or weaknesses that could help attackers gain access.

---

#### **3. Evaluate Attack Vectors**

* Determine possible attack paths.

---

#### **4. Identify Tools and Techniques**

* Consider how attackers would carry out the attack.

**Examples:**

* Phishing
* Malware
* Social engineering
* Exploits

---

## **Types of Threat Actors**

---

### **Overview**

* A **threat actor** is any person or group that presents a security risk.

* Threat actors have different:

  * Motivations
  * Skills
  * Resources

---

### **Cybercriminals**

* Commit attacks for:

  * Financial gain
  * Fraud
  * Theft

**Examples:**

* Ransomware groups
* Identity thieves

---

### **Hacktivists**

* Conduct attacks to promote:

  * Political beliefs
  * Social causes
  * Ideological goals

---

### **Insider Threats**

* Individuals within an organization who create security risks.

---

#### **Intentional Insider Threats**

* Deliberately harm the organization.

**Examples:**

* Data theft
* Sabotage
* Information leaks

---

#### **Unintentional Insider Threats**

* Accidentally create vulnerabilities.

**Examples:**

* Clicking phishing links
* Sharing sensitive information
* Misconfiguring systems

---

### **State-Sponsored Threat Actors**

* Supported by governments.

* Often highly skilled and well funded.

* May target:

  * Critical infrastructure
  * Government systems
  * Businesses

---

### **Script Kiddies**

* Individuals with limited technical knowledge.

* Use existing attack tools developed by others.

---

## **Pathways Through Defenses**

---

### **Overview**

* Attackers use **attack vectors** to bypass defenses.

* Security professionals identify attack vectors to reduce risk.

---

### **Attack Vector**

* A pathway used to penetrate security defenses.

---

### **Common Attack Vectors**

#### **Email**

* Phishing attacks
* Malware attachments

---

#### **Social Media**

* Information gathering
* Social engineering

---

#### **USB Devices**

* Malware delivery
* Unauthorized access

---

#### **Cloud Services**

* Misconfigured resources
* Exposed data

---

#### **Public-Facing Applications**

* Web applications
* Internet-facing services

---

### **Defending Against Attack Vectors**

#### **Security Awareness Training**

* Educates users about:

  * Phishing
  * Social engineering
  * Emerging threats

---

#### **Principle of Least Privilege (PoLP)**

* Users receive only the access they need.

---

#### **Security Tools**

* Antivirus software
* Firewalls
* Monitoring tools
* Access controls

---

### **Human Error**

* One of the most common causes of security incidents.

* Security controls help reduce risk, but user mistakes still occur.

---

## **Fortify Against Brute Force Cyber Attacks**

---

### **Overview**

* A **brute force attack** attempts to gain access by repeatedly guessing credentials.

* Attackers automate the process using software tools.

---

### **How Brute Force Attacks Work**

* Attackers repeatedly try:

  * Usernames
  * Passwords
  * Authentication combinations

* Goal:

  * Find valid credentials

---

### **Password Attacks**

#### **Dictionary Attack**

* Uses a list of common passwords and words.

---

#### **Credential Stuffing**

* Uses credentials stolen from previous breaches.

---

#### **Password Spraying**

* Attempts a few common passwords against many accounts.

---

### **Defenses Against Brute Force Attacks**

#### **Strong Password Policies**

* Long passwords
* Complex passwords
* Unique passwords

---

#### **Account Lockout Policies**

* Temporarily lock accounts after repeated failures.

---

#### **Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)**

* Requires additional verification.

* Makes stolen passwords less useful.

---

#### **Monitoring and Logging**

* Detect unusual login attempts.

* Alert security teams to suspicious activity.

---

## **Key Takeaways**

* An attack surface includes all vulnerabilities a threat actor could exploit.

* Security hardening reduces vulnerabilities and limits attack opportunities.

* Security professionals use an attacker mindset to proactively identify risks.

* Threat actors include cybercriminals, insiders, hacktivists, script kiddies, and nation-state attackers.

* Attack vectors are pathways attackers use to penetrate defenses.

* Common attack vectors include email, USB devices, social media, cloud services, and public-facing applications.

* Brute force attacks attempt to gain access through repeated credential guessing.

* Strong passwords, MFA, account lockouts, and monitoring help defend against brute force attacks.

---

## **Big Picture**

Cybersecurity professionals must think like attackers to effectively defend systems. By understanding threat actors, attack surfaces, attack vectors, and common attack methods such as brute force attacks, organizations can proactively reduce risk, strengthen defenses, and protect critical assets before attackers find opportunities to exploit them.
